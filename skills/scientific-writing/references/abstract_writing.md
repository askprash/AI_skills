# Writing High-Impact Abstracts

## Overview

The abstract is the most-read and least-revised part of a paper. Editors decide whether to send a manuscript out for review largely on the strength of its abstract; reviewers form their first impression from it; indexing services display it; and the overwhelming majority of readers will read *only* the abstract. A weak abstract caps the reach of even excellent work. The abstract therefore deserves disproportionate care: it must be a complete, self-contained, and compelling miniature of the paper.

This reference gives a concrete, sentence-by-sentence method for constructing abstracts, anchored on the *Nature* "summary paragraph" model — the most widely emulated template for high-impact, broad-audience abstracts — and supplemented by principles from established scientific-writing authorities (Schimel, *Writing Science*; Gopen & Swan, "The Science of Scientific Writing"; the AMA and APA style manuals; the CONSORT and PRISMA abstract extensions).

---

## The Gold Standard: The Nature Summary Paragraph

*Nature* asks authors to open each paper with a single "summary paragraph" of roughly 200 words (up to 300). Its design is the clearest published recipe for an abstract that is both rigorous and readable, and it transfers well to almost any journal. The paragraph performs **seven moves in a fixed order**, each with a target length:

| # | Move | Length | Audience / function |
|---|------|--------|---------------------|
| 1 | **Basic introduction** | 1–2 sentences | Comprehensible to a scientist in *any* discipline. Orient the complete outsider. |
| 2 | **Detailed background** | 2–3 sentences | Comprehensible to scientists in *related* disciplines. Narrow to the specific subfield and state the relevant prior consensus. |
| 3 | **The general problem** | 1 sentence | State clearly the specific problem this study addresses (often opens with "However,…"). |
| 4 | **The main result** | 1 sentence | Summarize the single central finding, signposted with "Here we show" or an equivalent. |
| 5 | **Results in context** | 2–3 sentences | Explain what the result reveals *relative to what was previously thought*, or how it adds to existing knowledge. |
| 6 | **General context** | 1–2 sentences | Put the results into a wider frame — the take-home message. |
| 7 | **Broader perspective** | 2–3 sentences (optional) | Comprehensible to an any-discipline scientist; include only if it materially improves the paper's accessibility. |

**Length targets.** Moves 1–6 should total ≈190 words. Adding move 7 brings the paragraph to ≈250 words. The absolute ceiling is **300 words**.

**Shape.** The paragraph is an **hourglass**: it opens wide (broad field), narrows to a single point (the specific problem and the one key result), then widens again (implications and perspective). Moves 1–3 funnel inward, move 4 is the waist, moves 5–7 funnel outward.

## Annotated Worked Example

This is the example *Nature* provides (an abstract from *Nature* **435**, 114–118), with each move marked:

> **[1 — Basic introduction]** During cell division, mitotic spindles are assembled by microtubule-based motor proteins. **[2 — Detailed background]** The bipolar organization of spindles is essential for proper segregation of chromosomes, and requires plus-end-directed homotetrameric motor proteins of the widely conserved kinesin-5 (BimC) family. Hypotheses for bipolar spindle formation include the 'push–pull mitotic muscle' model, in which kinesin-5 and opposing motor proteins act between overlapping microtubules. **[3 — General problem]** However, the precise roles of kinesin-5 during this process are unknown. **[4 — Main result]** Here we show that the vertebrate kinesin-5 Eg5 drives the sliding of microtubules depending on their relative orientation. **[5 — Results in context]** We found in controlled in vitro assays that Eg5 has the remarkable capability of simultaneously moving at ~20 nm s⁻¹ towards the plus-ends of each of the two microtubules it crosslinks. For anti-parallel microtubules, this results in relative sliding at ~40 nm s⁻¹, comparable to spindle pole separation rates in vivo. Furthermore, we found that Eg5 can tether microtubule plus-ends, suggesting an additional microtubule-binding mode for Eg5. **[6 — General context]** Our results demonstrate how members of the kinesin-5 family are likely to function in mitosis, pushing apart interpolar microtubules as well as recruiting microtubules into bundles that are subsequently polarized by relative sliding. **[7 — Broader perspective]** We anticipate our assay to be a starting point for more sophisticated in vitro models of mitotic spindles. For example, the individual and combined action of multiple mitotic motors could be tested, including minus-end-directed motors opposing Eg5 motility. Furthermore, Eg5 inhibition is a major target of anti-cancer drug development, and a well-defined and quantitative assay for motor function will be relevant for such developments.

Note that this example carries numbered reference citations — a *Nature*-family convention. See "Self-Containment Rules" below: the great majority of journals forbid citations in the abstract.

## The Abstract Is a Microcosm of the Paper

Joshua Schimel (*Writing Science*) argues that a well-told paper follows one of two narrative arcs, and that the abstract should reproduce that same arc in miniature:

- **OCAR — Opening, Challenge, Action, Resolution.** The default for specialist journals. The *Opening* frames the larger problem; the *Challenge* is the specific question or hypothesis; the *Action* is the 2–3 key results; the *Resolution* is the single take-home message.
- **LDR / LD — Lead, Development, (Resolution).** Favored by broad-audience journals (*Nature*, *Science*): the most important content — the key result — comes first, then is developed.

The Nature seven-move template *is* an OCAR structure: moves 1–2 are the Opening, move 3 is the Challenge, moves 4–5 are the Action, and moves 6–7 are the Resolution. Whichever arc the paper uses, the abstract must use the **same** arc. Critically, the abstract's Resolution must visibly answer the Challenge it posed, which must in turn address the problem raised in the Opening — a closed loop. If the last sentence does not connect back to the gap stated near the start, the abstract has not resolved.

**Mapping abstract elements to IMRAD:**

| Abstract move | IMRAD source | OCAR element |
|---------------|--------------|--------------|
| Basic introduction + background | Introduction (¶1–2) | Opening |
| The specific problem / gap | Introduction (gap paragraph) | Challenge |
| Main result + supporting results | Results | Action |
| Interpretation, context, perspective | Discussion / Conclusion | Resolution |

## Sentence-by-Sentence Construction Guide

### Move 1 — Basic introduction (1–2 sentences)
- Write for a scientist in *any* field — a chemist should be able to follow the first sentence of a neuroscience abstract.
- Open with an established, specific truth, not a cliché. Avoid "Since the dawn of time…", "X is an important problem", and dictionary-definition openings.
- Name the broad phenomenon and signal why it matters in a single move.

### Move 2 — Detailed background (2–3 sentences)
- Narrow to the specific subfield; field-specific terms are now acceptable (define any non-standard abbreviation).
- State what *is* known — the prior consensus the study will test, extend, or overturn. This sets up the contrast that move 5 will pay off.

### Move 3 — The problem (1 sentence)
- Exactly one sentence, and make it sharp. This is the Challenge; the entire paper exists to resolve it.
- It frequently opens with "However," "Yet," or "Despite this," because a gap is a contrast with the prior knowledge of move 2.
- Name a *specific* unknown ("the precise roles of kinesin-5 … are unknown"), never a vague one ("more work is needed").

### Move 4 — The main result (1 sentence)
- Use "Here we show that…" (or "we report," "we demonstrate," "we find"). This phrase is a load-bearing convention: it signals that the paper's contribution begins now.
- State the single most important finding — the one thing a reader should remember. Not methods, not a list of outcomes.
- Make it a *result*, not an activity: write "Here we show that X drives Y," not "Here we investigate the relationship between X and Y."
- **Methods, architecture, dataset, and software papers:** when the contribution is an artifact rather than a causal finding, state what the artifact *does* — its demonstrated capability or measured performance — not merely that it was built. Write "Here we show that [artifact] can [capability]" or "Here we present [artifact], which [achieves Y]," not "we developed [artifact]." The emphasis stays on a result the artifact produces.

### Move 5 — Results in context (2–3 sentences)
- This is the Action. Give the 2–3 supporting findings with concrete, quantitative specifics (rates, effect sizes, magnitudes, confidence intervals).
- Frame results against prior expectation where possible — anchoring a number to known biology ("comparable to spindle pole separation rates in vivo") makes it meaningful.
- Resist listing every result; select the ones that build the argument toward the conclusion.

### Move 6 — General context (1–2 sentences)
- Step back: what do the results, taken together, mean? This is the Resolution proper — the take-home message.
- Interpret, do not repeat: "Our results demonstrate how…", not a restatement of move 5.

### Move 7 — Broader perspective (2–3 sentences, optional)
- Future directions, applications, cross-disciplinary relevance. Include only if it genuinely widens the paper's reach; omit it to save words if space is tight.
- "We anticipate…" is the conventional opener.

## Word-Count Targets by Venue

| Venue type | Typical limit | Format |
|------------|---------------|--------|
| *Nature*, *Science* (summary paragraph) | ≈190 (moves 1–6); ≈250 with move 7; 300 max | Unstructured; numbered citations permitted |
| *Cell* and other broad-audience journals | 150–250 words | Unstructured |
| Field-specific journals | 200–300 words | Usually unstructured |
| Medical journals (JAMA, BMJ, NEJM, *Lancet*, *Annals*) | 250–350 words | **Structured** (labeled headings required) |
| ML / CS conferences (NeurIPS, ICML, ACL) | 150–250 words | Unstructured |

Always check the target journal's author guidelines: the word limit and the structured/unstructured choice are non-negotiable journal rules, not stylistic preferences.

A complete seven-move summary paragraph naturally runs ≈190 words (moves 1–6) to ≈250 words (with move 7) — these are the figures from *Nature*'s own annotated example. When a journal sets a shorter limit, compress in this order: cut move 7 first, then trim background detail in moves 1–2 and qualifiers throughout. Never drop the problem, the main result, or the core supporting findings (moves 3–6). Do not aim below ≈150 words with all seven moves intact — the per-move sentence budget cannot be met at that length.

## Two Formats: Flowing vs. Structured

### Flowing (unstructured) — the default
Write the seven moves as one cohesive paragraph (occasionally two) with natural transitions and no labels. This is correct for *Nature*, *Science*, *Cell*, PNAS, ML/CS conferences, and most field journals. The seven-move structure governs the *abstract* at every venue, including ML and CS conferences; numbered contribution lists ("Our contributions are: (1)… (2)…"), common in those fields, belong in the *Introduction*, not the abstract. See `imrad_structure.md` for a worked WRONG/CORRECT comparison.

### Structured — only when the journal requires it
Many clinical journals mandate labeled sections (Background/Objective, Methods, Results, Conclusions; CONSORT and PRISMA define extended label sets for trials and systematic reviews). Use labels *only* when the author guidelines require them. Even then, write each labeled section in complete sentences — never fragments — and apply the same seven-move logic within the labels.

## Sentence-Level Craft

Drawing on Gopen & Swan's "The Science of Scientific Writing," abstract sentences should observe these reader-expectation principles:

- **Topic position / stress position.** Readers expect familiar, linking information at the *start* of a sentence and the new, emphasis-worthy information at the *end*. Place the result, the number, or the point you want remembered in the stress position.
- **Keep subject and verb close together.** A reader holds a sentence open until the verb arrives; long subject–verb gaps tax working memory.
- **One main idea per sentence.** Abstracts have no room for three layers of stacked subordinate clauses.
- **Front-load the contribution.** For broad-audience journals especially, the key result should appear early; never bury "Here we show" deep in a long paragraph.
- **Prefer concrete specifics over vague claims.** "reduced infections by 47%" beats "substantially reduced infections."
- **Use active voice and strong verbs:** "We show," "X drives Y," "the model predicts."
- **Match tense to move:** present tense for established facts (moves 1–2) and for conclusions (moves 6–7); past tense for what you did and what you found (moves 4–5).

## Self-Containment Rules

The abstract must stand completely alone — it is indexed, displayed, and read in isolation from the paper.

**Exclude:**
- Citations and reference numbers — *except* in *Nature*-family journals, whose summary paragraphs do use numbered citations. For essentially all other journals: no citations.
- References to figures, tables, equations, or sections ("see Figure 2").
- Undefined abbreviations and acronyms — spell out at first use, or avoid them. Abbreviate only terms used three or more times within the abstract itself. Field-standard abbreviations and metric names that the target audience never sees written in full (e.g., DNA, PCR, BLEU) are exempt and need no definition.
- Any information, result, or conclusion not present in the main text.
- Hype words used as a substitute for evidence ("novel," "unprecedented," "first-ever," "paradigm-shifting") — let the result speak.

**Include:**
- Every element a reader needs to understand the contribution without the paper.
- The key quantitative results, with effect sizes and confidence intervals where applicable.
- Enough method detail to establish credibility (study design, sample size, key technique) — without a methods deep-dive.

## Common Failure Modes

| Failure | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Abstract is the Introduction's first paragraph copy-pasted | Write the abstract last, from scratch, as a synthesis of the *whole* paper. |
| No result stated — only "we investigate X" | Every abstract must state what was *found*, not merely what was studied. |
| Vague results ("significant improvements were observed") | Give numbers: magnitudes, percentages, effect sizes, confidence intervals. |
| Buried contribution | Move the key result into a single, early "Here we show" sentence. |
| Opens too broad or with a cliché | Start with a specific, true statement about the actual subfield. |
| Problem (Challenge) missing or vague | Add one sharp sentence naming the *specific* unknown. |
| Methods overwhelm the abstract | Compress methods to one or two clauses; spend the words on results. |
| Conclusion overclaims | Hedge appropriately; the abstract's claim must match both the data and the Discussion. |
| Resolution does not answer the Challenge | Re-read: the closing sentences must close the loop opened in move 3. |
| Over the word limit | Cut move 7 first; then trim qualifiers and redundant background. |

## Quality Checklist

Run every abstract against this before finalizing:

**Structure**
- [ ] Performs the moves in order: basic introduction → detailed background → specific problem → main result → results in context → general context → optional broader perspective
- [ ] Contains a single "Here we show"-type sentence stating the one key finding
- [ ] Resolution closes the loop with the Challenge and the Opening
- [ ] Has an hourglass shape: broad → narrow → broad

**Content**
- [ ] First sentence is comprehensible to a scientist in any discipline
- [ ] Key results are quantitative and specific
- [ ] No claim exceeds what the data support
- [ ] Fully self-contained — understandable without the paper

**Compliance**
- [ ] Within the journal's word limit
- [ ] Correct format (flowing vs. structured) per author guidelines
- [ ] No citations (unless a *Nature*-family journal); no figure/table references
- [ ] Abbreviations defined at first use or avoided
- [ ] Verb tense correct for each move

**Craft**
- [ ] Flowing prose — no bullet points, no sentence fragments
- [ ] Active voice and strong verbs
- [ ] Important information sits in sentence stress positions
- [ ] Reads well aloud; transitions between moves are smooth

## Writing Process: Applying the Two Stages to the Abstract

**Stage 1 — Outline.** Write the abstract *after* the full paper is drafted. List the raw material for each of the seven moves as bullet notes:

```
- [1] Field: hospital-acquired infections — major preventable harm + cost
- [2] Background: surfaces are a pathogen reservoir; manual cleaning leaves gaps
- [3] Problem: does adjunctive UV-C disinfection cut clinical infection rates? unknown
- [4] Main result: Here we show UV-C lowers both contamination and infections
- [5] Results: contamination -47% (CI 38-56%, p<0.001); CAUTI 3.2->1.8/1000 cath-days
       (RR 0.56, CI 0.38-0.83, p=0.004); no adverse events
- [6] Context: UV-C addresses the residual burden manual cleaning leaves
- [7] Perspective: quantitative surface-level evidence aids infection-control prioritization
```

**Stage 2 — Prose.** Convert the bullets into one flowing paragraph with transitions, never leaving the bullets as the final form:

> **[1]** Hospital-acquired infections remain a leading cause of preventable morbidity and rising healthcare costs worldwide. **[2]** Environmental surfaces are a recognized reservoir for nosocomial pathogens, and standard manual cleaning leaves a substantial fraction of high-touch surfaces contaminated. **[3]** However, whether adding automated ultraviolet-C (UV-C) disinfection to standard protocols reduces clinical infection rates remains unclear. **[4]** Here we show, in a 12-month before-after study in a 500-bed teaching hospital, that adjunctive UV-C disinfection lowers both surface contamination and infection incidence. **[5]** Monthly surface cultures revealed a 47% reduction in contamination (95% CI, 38–56%; p<0.001), and catheter-associated urinary tract infections fell from 3.2 to 1.8 per 1,000 catheter-days (RR=0.56; 95% CI, 0.38–0.83; p=0.004), with no adverse events. **[6]** These findings indicate that UV-C disinfection meaningfully reduces the environmental pathogen burden that manual cleaning leaves behind. **[7]** We anticipate that quantitative, surface-level evidence of this kind will help hospitals prioritize automated disinfection within infection-control programs.

This revised abstract is ~150 words, performs all seven moves, states a single clear result, reports specific quantitative findings, and closes the loop between the problem and the resolution.

## Before / After Revision

**Before (weak):**
> In this paper we investigate hospital infections, which are a big problem. We did a study using UV-C light. We collected data over a year. The results were good and showed improvements. We believe UV-C is a novel approach that could be useful. This will be discussed further below.

Problems: no specific field framing; no stated gap; "we investigate" with no result; vague findings ("good," "improvements"); hype word ("novel"); self-reference ("in this paper," "discussed below"); no numbers; no resolution.

**After:** see the seven-move revision in the section above — same study, transformed into a self-contained, quantitative, hourglass-shaped abstract.
