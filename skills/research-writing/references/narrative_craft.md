# Narrative Craft — Story Structure for Scientific Manuscripts

This is the **story layer** that sits underneath IMRAD. IMRAD (and the AIAA numbered-section
template) is a *rule* — a container. The principle that makes the container hold a reader is
**story structure**. A paper that satisfies IMRAD but has no narrative arc is a data dump;
it gets read by no one and cited by fewer.

Adapted from Joshua Schimel, *Writing Science: How to Write Papers That Get Cited and
Proposals That Get Funded* (Oxford Univ. Press, 2011). Pair this with `house_style.md` (the
domain voice), `writing_principles.md` (sentence-level craft and revision), and
`imrad_structure.md` (the section container). Where this conflicts with a journal's hard
requirement, the journal wins.

---

## 0. First principles

- **The goal is not to be published; it is to be read, understood, and cited.** Write
  *effectively*, not merely correctly.
- **A paper is a story, not a report of what you did.** The data are supporting actors; the
  lead actors are the question and the larger problem. Let the story grow from the data —
  never decide the story first and jam data into it.
- **Push every paper up the chain `Data → Information → Knowledge → Understanding`.** Stopping
  at data or information leaves the reader to find the point themselves; most won't.
- **It is the author's job to make the reader's job easy.** This is the overriding principle;
  every rule below is a way of honoring it.

## 1. SUCCES — the stickiness rubric

Use both to draft and to critique. A sticky paper is:

| | Element | In a paper |
|---|---|---|
| **S** | Simple | Captures the essential core. *Simple* ≠ *simplistic* (which dodges the core). |
| **U** | Unexpected | Frames a knowledge gap — the "unknown within the known" — so the reader feels "I didn't realize we didn't know that." |
| **C** | Concrete | Specific objects, numbers, mechanisms — not abstractions floating in the middle of the ladder of abstraction. |
| **C** | Credible | An unbroken chain: ideas → methods → data → conclusions. One break sinks all of it; hype and buzzwords destroy credibility. |
| **E** | Emotional | The only admissible scientific emotion is **curiosity**. Create it by posing the gap as a question. |
| **S** | Stories | The whole thing has a beginning, middle, and end — an arc, not a list. |

## 2. OCAR — the spine

Every story (paper, proposal, section, paragraph, sentence) is built from four elements:

| Element | The question it answers | In a paper |
|---|---|---|
| **O — Opening** | Who/what is this about? What is the *larger problem*? | Frames the broad problem and the main "characters." |
| **C — Challenge** | What specific question must be answered? | The hypotheses / questions / objectives. |
| **A — Action** | What happened to address it? | Methods, Results, most of the Discussion. |
| **R — Resolution** | How has our understanding of the world changed? | The conclusion that answers the Challenge and re-widens to the Opening. |

- A story missing **any** OCAR element reads as "unsatisfying, ineffective, and slippery,
  rather than sticky." A paper with no Resolution "falls into the reject bin."
- **OCAR is fractal:** it applies at every scale — whole paper, section, paragraph, sentence.
- **The arc is a spiral, not a circle:** the Resolution returns to the Opening's problem, but
  the reader's understanding has *moved*.

## 3. The four structures — choose by audience patience

Same OCAR elements, different assembly order. The choice is governed by **how patient the
audience is** — will they wait for the point, or do they want it now?

| Structure | Shape | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| **OCAR** | O→C→A→R in order; Challenge ends the intro, conclusion ends the paper. | **Specialist journals.** Patient expert readers. A front-loaded lead here looks *suspect* — as if you forced the data to fit a pre-decided story. |
| **LDR** | Lead (O+C+R compressed) → Development → Resolution. | **Generalist / high-impact journals** (*Nature*, *Science*). Editors triage fast; you need a strong lead just to be sent for review. Watson & Crick (1953) is the canonical science LDR. |
| **ABDCE** | Action, Background, Development, Climax, Ending (*in medias res*). | **Proposals** and impatient panels who may judge by page one. Hook first, then back up to fill in Background. |
| **LD** | "Inverted pyramid": whole story in the lead, then development. | Newspaper style; the logic matters for **titles and abstracts**. |

Rule of thumb: **patient expert audience → OCAR; impatient editor/broad audience →
front-load (LDR/ABDCE).** Readers identify O, C, and R by their *positions* — they read
whatever sits in each slot as your opening, challenge, and resolution, so a reader skimming
only those three should still get the key points.

## 4. OCAR ↔ IMRAD, and the hourglass

- **Introduction** = **Opening** (usually the first paragraph) + Background (an extension of
  the Opening, fleshing out the characters — hence OCAR, not OBCAR) + **Challenge** (the
  specific questions, ending the intro).
- **Methods + Results** = the **Action**.
- **Discussion** = the rest of the Action building to the climax, then the **Resolution**
  (often a final Conclusions paragraph).

The paper has an **hourglass shape**: open wide (a problem of broad interest) → narrow to the
neck (Challenge, Methods, Results) → widen back out (Resolution reconnects to the Opening).

**Match the hourglass: opening width ≈ resolution width.**
- Opening wider than the resolution = **over-promising**; readers feel cheated (the worst error).
- Resolution wider than the opening = **under-selling**; readers who'd have cared never start.
- If you must err, err **slightly wide** — you can filter down quickly, but a too-narrow
  opening turns the audience away before they begin.

## 5. The Opening

The first sentence/paragraph is a **power position**; first impressions are strong and
lasting. A good opening does three jobs: **(1) identify the driving problem, (2) introduce the
characters, (3) signal the target audience** (who should read this and how to think about it).
If you can, also foreshadow the Challenge and the conclusion.

Two ways openings fail — both are failures to give **direction**:
- **Misdirection** — the first sentence points one way, the next paragraph goes another.
  *Fix:* broaden the first sentence to a positioning statement that encompasses both threads.
- **No direction** — the opening rehearses textbook material the audience already holds, so
  they can't tell what the paper is about. *Fix on revision:* find where the real story starts
  and delete everything before it.

The first sentence is a deliberate move, not a platitude:
- **Pawn push** — an understated positioning sentence that sets up the next move. Fits
  OCAR / specialist journals.
- **Queen launch** — dive straight in with a dramatic statement. Fits LDR/ABDCE, *Nature*, NIH.
- **Two-step opening** — for a broad audience that doesn't share your schema: open on an issue
  that engages them, then modulate to your narrow topic. Like a dance, keep it to **two steps**
  — more and you stumble.

## 6. The Funnel — connecting Opening to Challenge

The body of the Introduction narrows from the broad Opening to the specific Challenge,
**framing the knowledge gap** along the way.

- **An Introduction describes ignorance, not knowledge.** A literature review builds a wall of
  what we know; an Introduction shows the *hole* in the wall. Narrow stepwise — each step names
  what we know only to mark the *edge* of that knowledge.
- **Diagnostic — check the sentence subject.** "**X occurs (Smith 2003)**" is a story about
  nature (good). "**Smith (2003) found X**" is a story about Smith — a data dump that signals
  you haven't synthesized yet. (*Exception:* foreground the researcher when narrating a genuine
  debate.)
- **Ban "little is known about X."** It is usually false, self-contradicting (you then describe
  a lot), and fuzzy. State *concretely* what we don't know.
- **Avoid the "bizzwidget" error** — selling a solution/method before establishing the problem.
  To sell a solution, first sell the problem: define it, show why existing tools fall short,
  *then* introduce your answer.
- **Nothing belongs in the Funnel that doesn't move the story forward.** If a fact has no
  concrete role connecting the Opening to the Challenge, cut it.

By the time the reader reaches the Challenge, your questions should feel like the obvious ones.

## 7. The Challenge

The Challenge states the **specific knowledge you hope to gain** — the question that drove the
work. It is the story's tension; everything after is the attempt to resolve it.

- **Lead with the question, not the data.** A strong Challenge condenses to **"to learn X, we
  did Y."** The load-bearing part is **"to learn X"** (the knowledge gap), not "we did Y" (the
  objectives). The single most common failure is stating objectives where a question belongs —
  "weak science and weak storytelling."
- **Tie the narrow question back to the big problem** from the Opening.
- **Order objectives by importance, not chronology.** Lead with the intellectually interesting
  question in **strong verbs** ("evaluate how…"); subordinate methods-validation goals in
  weaker verbs ("examine," "compare"). Verb strength signals the hierarchy.
- A **hypothesis** is an optional tool that turns the question into a falsifiable prediction;
  the question is still the key. Make hypotheses explicit and concrete, never implied.

> Weak: "Here we examined the structural characteristics of anergic T-cells and tested their
> response…" (data only). Strong: "*To determine what causes T-cells to be anergic,* we
> evaluated the structural characteristics of T-cells and how they responded…"

## 8. The Action

The Action (Methods, Results, much of the Discussion) is **a developing story, not a data
dump.** Let the Challenge decide what to report and in what order.

- **Choose which data to present, not just how.** You collected more than the story needs.
  **"Murder your darlings"** — cut data that don't advance the story (archive *all* of it in a
  repository, but keep the narrative clean). You can only make these cuts once you know the story.
- **Present data before the inferences drawn from them.** Conclusions-before-data feels like
  you are "imposing plot." Mark the move from data to inference with words like *indicates* /
  *demonstrates*. Keep what you **found** distinguishable from what you **think**.
- **Lead each result with the pattern, then the detail (LD structure).** Don't bury the pattern
  under numbers.
- **Methods open with the goal, then the procedure,** and tap named/standard methods ("measured
  protein by the Lowry approach"), describing only deviations.
- **Report the effect size, not just significance.** "Warming increased emissions by a factor
  of 3.4 (*p* < 0.05)" — never merely "significantly increased." The story is in the *amount*;
  the *p*-value is interpretation, not the result.

## 9. The Resolution

Endings are power positions — the reader remembers the last thing you say. The Resolution is
your take-home message; don't blow the punchline. A good one does three things, concretely:

1. **Synopsize** the key results.
2. **Synthesize** — show how those results answer the Challenge.
3. **Contextualize** — show what this contributes to the Opening's larger problem (re-widen the
   hourglass, close the spiral back to the Opening).

**Summarizing is not resolving.** Restating data without synthesizing it into new knowledge is
a weak ending.

Three ways to wreck a Resolution — and what a critique should flag:
- **Weak** — asserts importance without saying *how/why*. Fix: state the concrete new insight.
- **Distracting** — ends on a truism, or springs a **brand-new idea** never set up earlier.
  "Plot twists are forbidden" — weave late insights back into the Introduction instead.
- **Self-undermining** — the worst and most common: **"more research is needed,"** or fuzzy
  hedges that use the power position to weaken your own science. Uncertainties are real, but the
  Resolution is not where they go.

Ending on a **question** is fine *if* it is concrete and grew from your work (it extends the
conclusions) — that is the opposite of "more research is needed," which is a question *about*
the work. **Proposals need a resolution paragraph too** — reviewers often decide at the end;
re-encapsulate the pitch and end strong.

## 10. Internal structure — the arc recurses

OCAR is fractal. Apply it below the paper level:

- **Every section, subsection, and paragraph is its own complete arc** (opening → development →
  resolution), nested inside the larger arc.
- **Schema + power positions.** Readers learn by slotting new information into a framework they
  already hold (a *schema*) — build the context first, then deliver the point. Beginnings and
  endings are power positions; put the key point at a boundary, never buried mid-arc.
- **One idea per unit; resolve it before moving on.** The #1 failure is stream-of-consciousness
  prose that keeps adding new directions and never closes back to its opening.
- **A paragraph is a unit of thought — a complete short story.** Don't break it where you "come
  up for air." Use the last (stress) sentence to *land the point*, not merely to transition.
- **Point-first dominates (~70–75%).** Topic-sentence-first (TS-D) and LD are point-first; use
  them for most paragraphs. Point-last (LDR/OCAR) belongs at openings, resolutions, and
  transitions. The topic sentence need not be the first sentence or a single sentence.

For sentence-level craft (character-in-subject / action-in-verb, topic vs. stress positions,
the given→new contract, active voice, fuzzy verbs, zombie nouns), condensing, and the
editing-pass order — see **`writing_principles.md`**.

## Quick checklist — structuring a paper

1. Pick the structure by audience patience: specialist → **OCAR**; *Nature/Science* → **LDR**;
   proposal → **ABDCE**.
2. Place O, C, R in their expected slots so each reads correctly in isolation.
3. Open in the power position; do all three jobs (problem, characters, audience); foreshadow C/R.
4. Match opening width to resolution width (hourglass); if unsure, err slightly wide.
5. Build a stepwise **Funnel** that frames a concrete knowledge gap and ends on a concrete
   Challenge ("to learn X, we did Y").
6. Tell the **Action** as a story — pattern before detail, data before inference, effect sizes,
   darlings murdered.
7. End with a **Resolution** that synopsizes → synthesizes → contextualizes and closes the spiral.

## Quick checklist — critiquing a draft's story

- Opening gives single clear **direction** (not misdirection, not "no direction")?
- Introduction defines **ignorance** (a gap), not a wall of **knowledge** (lit review)? Check
  sentence subjects ("X occurs" vs. "Smith found"). No "little is known," no bizzwidget?
- Is the **Challenge** an explicit question ("to learn X"), not just objectives?
- Is the **Action** a story (effect sizes, data-before-inference) or a data dump?
- Does the **Resolution** close the circle at the opening's width, and avoid the three killers
  (weak / distracting / "more research is needed")?
- Does every section and paragraph have its own arc with the point in a power position?
