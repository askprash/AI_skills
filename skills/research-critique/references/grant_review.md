# Grant Review

Grant review evaluates *proposed* work — claims about feasibility, significance, and the team's capacity — rather than completed results. The rubrics differ significantly from manuscript review.

## How grant review differs from manuscript review

- **You're evaluating a plan, not results.** Feasibility and reasoning take the place of demonstrated execution.
- **Significance is a primary criterion.** "Sound but not very important" can sink a strong proposal.
- **The team matters.** Track record, environment, and resources are evaluated, not just the science.
- **Preliminary data is a feature, not a publication.** Don't ask for it to be tied up to publishable standards.
- **Brevity is enforced.** Pages are limited; you can't fault the absence of detail that wouldn't fit.
- **The review is for a panel, not authors.** Many funders don't send reviews to applicants (or send only edited versions). Write for the program officer and panel.

## NIH review

The dominant grant rubric in biomedical research worldwide. Applicants are scored on five core criteria, plus overall impact.

### Five core review criteria

1. **Significance** — Is the problem important? Will the work change concepts, methods, technologies, or services? Even if successful, would it advance the field?
2. **Investigator(s)** — Are the PIs and team well suited (training, expertise, track record)? Do team members have complementary expertise? Is there reasonable evidence they can execute?
3. **Innovation** — Does the work challenge or shift paradigms? Are concepts, methods, or instrumentation novel? Refining or applying existing approaches in new ways counts but is less highly scored than novel approaches.
4. **Approach** — Are the strategy, methods, and analyses well-reasoned, appropriate, and feasible? Are pitfalls anticipated with alternative strategies? Are timelines realistic? Are statistical methods appropriate? Is the experimental design rigorous (sex as a biological variable, authentication of key biological resources, scientific rigor)?
5. **Environment** — Are the institutional facilities, equipment, and resources adequate? Will the environment contribute to probability of success?

Each scored 1 (exceptional) to 9 (poor). Overall impact score is **not** an average; it's a holistic judgment of likely impact.

### Additional review considerations (not scored, but commented on)

- Protections for human subjects (including inclusion of women, minorities, children).
- Vertebrate animals (justified species/numbers/procedures, 3Rs, euthanasia).
- Biohazards.
- Resource sharing plan.
- Authentication of key biological and chemical resources.
- Budget appropriateness (commented but doesn't drive score).

### NIH-specific concerns

- **Rigor and reproducibility section**: required, evaluated under Approach. Look for: scientific premise (rigor of prior work), rigorous experimental design, consideration of relevant biological variables (sex, age, etc.), authentication of resources.
- **Inclusion plan**: Inclusion across the lifespan, inclusion of women and minorities. NIH expects either inclusion or strong scientific justification for exclusion.
- **Specific Aims structure**: One page. Independent but synergistic aims. Aim 3 doesn't depend on Aim 1 succeeding.
- **Preliminary Data**: For R01-type grants, expected to support feasibility and prior expertise.
- **Mechanism**: R01 (project), R21 (exploratory/developmental), R03 (small grant), R34 (planning), K (career development), F (fellowship), T (training), U (cooperative) all have different expectations. Don't apply R01 standards to an R21.

### How to write an NIH review

Per criterion, list strengths and weaknesses, then justify a score. Aim for substantive, specific feedback. Avoid vague language ("the project is interesting" is unhelpful).

Common scoring rubric:
- 1–3: high impact, well-justified.
- 4–6: moderate impact, mixed strengths/weaknesses.
- 7–9: low impact, major concerns.

## NSF review

Two primary criteria:

1. **Intellectual Merit** — Importance and potential of the proposed activity to advance knowledge. Creative, original, transformative concepts. Qualifications of the PI and team. Soundness of the rationale and plan. Adequacy of resources.

2. **Broader Impacts** — Potential to benefit society and contribute to specific desired outcomes. Examples: full participation of underrepresented groups, STEM workforce development, improved STEM education, infrastructure for research and education, partnerships, public scientific literacy, well-being of society, national security, economic competitiveness.

Both criteria are weighed in five elements:
- Potential to advance knowledge / benefit society
- Creative/original/transformative concepts
- Plan well-reasoned, well-organized, based on sound rationale
- Qualifications of PI/team
- Adequacy of resources

### NSF-specific concerns

- **Solicitation requirements**: NSF programs have specific solicitations with their own additional criteria. Always check the solicitation.
- **Data Management Plan**: 2 pages max, evaluated as part of Intellectual Merit.
- **Postdoc Mentoring Plan**: Required if postdocs are included in budget.
- **Results from prior NSF support**: Required if PI had NSF funding in the past 5 years.

### How to write an NSF review

Two sections: Intellectual Merit and Broader Impacts. For each, list strengths and weaknesses. Provide a summary statement and a rating (Excellent, Very Good, Good, Fair, Poor).

## ERC review

European Research Council. Three main schemes: Starting (StG), Consolidator (CoG), Advanced (AdG). Two-step process (Step 1: extended synopsis; Step 2: full proposal + interview).

### Evaluation criteria

1. **Research Project (groundbreaking nature, ambition and feasibility)** — Is the proposed research at the frontier? Are the objectives well-formulated and ambitious? Are the methodology and feasibility credible? Are the impacts beyond current state-of-the-art?

2. **Principal Investigator (intellectual capacity, creativity, commitment)** — Track record commensurate with career stage. Major publications without senior PI. Other significant contributions. Independence.

Both criteria weighed in scoring (A, B, C; A/A required to be funded at Step 2).

### ERC-specific concerns

- **Frontier research** is the core concept; incremental work is generally not funded.
- **High risk / high gain** is welcomed.
- **Mono-disciplinary or interdisciplinary** are both fine, but interdisciplinary should be well-integrated.
- **Independence and leadership** at the PI's career stage is emphasized.
- **Track-record portion** weighs heavily; mid-career and senior schemes (CoG, AdG) require substantial independent contributions.

## NHMRC review

Australia's National Health and Medical Research Council. Schemes include Investigator Grants, Ideas Grants, Synergy Grants, Partnership Grants.

### Investigator Grants

Two criteria:
1. **Research quality and impact** (track record, weighted by career stage).
2. **Knowledge gain / significance of contribution to the field**.

### Ideas Grants

Four criteria:
1. **Research quality** (track record, capability).
2. **Innovation and creativity**.
3. **Significance**.
4. **Feasibility**.

NHMRC also evaluates ethics (human/animal), gender equity, and Aboriginal and Torres Strait Islander research where relevant.

## Wellcome and other UK funders

### Wellcome

Wellcome funds research that improves health. Schemes include Discovery Awards, Career Development Awards, Early-Career Awards, Mental Health Awards, etc. Each scheme has specific criteria but common themes:

- Research vision and approach.
- Researcher (track record, environment, support).
- Outputs and impact.
- Equity, diversity, and inclusion increasingly weighted.

### UKRI and Research Councils (EPSRC, BBSRC, MRC, NERC, AHRC, ESRC, STFC)

Each has its own criteria. Common elements:

- Research quality and importance.
- Researcher capability.
- Resources and management.
- Outputs, dissemination, impact.

UK funders emphasize **impact plans** (academic and beyond-academic impact, knowledge exchange, public engagement).

## Common pitfalls in grant proposals

### Significance

- Importance not articulated to a broad reviewer (assume the reviewer is in your field but not your sub-sub-field).
- "Significant" used as filler without substance.
- The "so what" of the proposed work isn't clear.

### Innovation

- Innovation claimed but the approach is a routine application of standard methods.
- Innovation overclaimed (genuinely incremental work described as transformative).
- Innovation underclaimed (genuinely novel work described too modestly).

### Approach

- Aim 3 depends on Aim 1 succeeding (interdependence creates risk).
- No alternative strategies / contingencies for predictable obstacles.
- Methods listed without justification.
- Statistical plan is afterthought.
- Sample size or power calculation absent.
- Sex/age/relevant-variable consideration missing (esp. NIH).
- Timeline unrealistic.
- Preliminary data weak or absent (for grants where it's expected).

### Investigators and Environment

- Team composition doesn't cover the expertise needed.
- Letters of support are generic.
- Environment description is boilerplate.
- Career-stage mismatch for the scheme (e.g., applying for a senior grant without senior independence track record).

### Innovation vs. Approach tension

A common issue: very innovative proposals often have weaker preliminary data and less detailed approach. Reviewers should calibrate — a Discovery / Exploratory scheme tolerates this; a project-grant scheme like R01 doesn't.

### Beyond-science criteria

- Inclusion plans absent or perfunctory (esp. NIH).
- Broader impacts treated as afterthought (esp. NSF).
- EDI and engagement plans rote rather than thoughtful (UK funders).
- Data management plan boilerplate.

## Tone and approach

Grant reviews should be:

- **Constructive, even when negative.** Reviewers are gatekeepers but also coaches.
- **Specific about why a weakness matters.** "Approach unclear" is not useful; "Aim 2, Section C.4: the proposed CRISPR screen design lacks negative controls and a clear path to validate hits" is.
- **Balanced.** Strengths and weaknesses. A review that's all positive or all negative is harder for the panel to use.
- **Calibrated to the scheme.** Don't demand R01-level detail from an R21. Don't demand certainty from an exploratory grant.
- **Not personal.** Critique the proposal, not the person.

## Writing the grant review

1. **Read the entire proposal first**, including Specific Aims, before forming a strong opinion.
2. **Identify what's being claimed** as significance, innovation, feasibility.
3. **Identify what would have to be true** for the work to succeed.
4. **Identify what would have to be true** for the work to matter if successful.
5. **Map findings to the funder's criteria.**
6. **Write criterion by criterion**, with specific strengths and weaknesses.
7. **Calibrate the overall score** with the comments. Reviewers often write critical comments but score generously, or vice versa. Coherence helps panels.
